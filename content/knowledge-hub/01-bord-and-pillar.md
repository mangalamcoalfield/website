# Bord & pillar underground coal mining, explained

Most people picture coal mining as a great open pit carved into a hillside. But a large share of India's coal — especially the deeper, higher-quality coking coal of the Jharia coalfield — lies too far below the surface to dig from above. To reach it, you go underground. And the oldest, most widely used method of underground coal mining in India has a deceptively simple name: **bord and pillar**.

## The basic idea

Imagine you want to remove coal from a flat seam buried underground, but you also need the roof above that seam to stay up while people and machines work beneath it. Bord and pillar solves both problems at once.

Miners drive a grid of tunnels — called **bords** (or galleries) — into the coal seam, running in two directions so they cross each other at right angles. What is left behind, between the tunnels, is a regular pattern of **pillars** of solid coal. Those pillars are not waste. They are doing a job: holding up the roof. The result, seen from above in plan, looks like a chessboard — open squares (the extracted galleries) separated by solid squares (the coal pillars).

This first phase is called **development**. You have opened up the seam, created access and ventilation routes, and extracted a portion of the coal — but a large fraction is still standing as support.

## Getting the rest of the coal

The coal locked in those pillars can be very valuable, and in many mines a second phase follows, called **depillaring** or pillar extraction. Working in a planned, retreating sequence — generally from the far end of a district back towards the access roads — miners reduce or remove the pillars and allow the roof behind them to collapse in a controlled way into the void (the **goaf**). Done correctly, the ground subsides where it is meant to, and the workers are always positioned in supported ground ahead of the collapse line.

Depillaring is the more hazardous phase, because you are deliberately removing the very supports that hold up the roof. It demands careful planning, strict sequencing, and continuous attention to how the ground is behaving. In India it is tightly governed by regulation and supervised by qualified statutory officials.

## Why pillars, and how big?

The size of the pillars and the width of the galleries are not guesswork. They are engineered from the depth of the seam, the strength of the coal and the surrounding rock, and the load the pillars must carry. Deeper seams carry more weight from the ground above, so they need bigger pillars. Get this wrong in the unsafe direction and you risk a **bump** — the sudden failure of a pillar or pillars under load — or a roof fall. Get it wrong in the over-cautious direction and you leave recoverable coal in the ground forever. Sound pillar design is the heart of the method.

## Bord and pillar vs. longwall

The other major underground method is **longwall mining**, where a single long face of coal — often a couple of hundred metres wide — is sheared away in one continuous operation while hydraulic supports hold the roof immediately above the machine and advance with it, letting the roof collapse behind. Longwall can achieve very high output from the right geology, but it needs large, regular, undisturbed blocks of coal to be worthwhile.

Bord and pillar, by contrast, is flexible. It copes with irregular boundaries, faulted ground, and the complex, much-worked geology typical of old Indian collieries. It can be operated with anything from manual and conventional equipment to modern continuous miners. That flexibility is exactly why it remains the workhorse method for reviving historic underground workings.

## Why this matters for reviving old mines

When an established underground colliery is brought back into operation, the existing layout is usually a bord-and-pillar grid developed by earlier generations of miners. Reviving it means re-establishing safe access, ventilation and ground support through those old galleries, dewatering where workings have flooded, and re-planning extraction within the safety rules that apply today. Understanding the grid — what was developed, what pillars remain, how the ground will behave — is the first technical task of any revival.

## In short

Bord and pillar means cutting a grid of tunnels and leaving coal pillars to hold up the roof, then — where planned and permitted — recovering those pillars in a controlled retreat. It is simple in concept, demanding in execution, and the foundation of underground coal mining in India.

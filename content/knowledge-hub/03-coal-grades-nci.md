# Coal grades & the National Coal Index, explained

Not all coal is the same, and not all coal is sold the same way. Before coal can be priced, it has to be **graded** — sorted by what it is good for and how much usable energy it carries. And to track how coal prices move across the country as a whole, India publishes a single benchmark: the **National Coal Index**. This article explains both.

## Two big families: thermal and coking

Coal is broadly used for two very different purposes, and the distinction matters enormously for grading and price.

**Thermal coal** (also called non-coking or steam coal) is burned to release heat — chiefly to raise steam in power stations. What buyers care about is how much heat a tonne delivers.

**Coking coal** (or metallurgical coal) is used to make **coke**, the carbon fuel and reducing agent at the heart of steelmaking in a blast furnace. Here, simply giving off heat is not enough — the coal must have the special "caking" property of softening, swelling and re-solidifying into strong, porous coke. Good coking coal is scarcer and more valuable than thermal coal, and India imports a great deal of it. The Jharia coalfield, where Amlabad sits, is historically India's most important source of prime coking coal.

## How thermal coal is graded: GCV bands

Indian thermal coal is graded by its **Gross Calorific Value (GCV)** — the total heat energy released when a unit of coal is burned, measured in kilocalories per kilogram (kcal/kg). The system runs in bands: the highest band is **GCV exceeding 7,000 kcal/kg**, and the bands step down from there (6,700–7,000, 6,400–6,700, and so on) all the way to the lowest grades below about 2,200 kcal/kg. The higher the GCV, the better the grade and, ordinarily, the higher the notified price. Grading by GCV replaced the older "Useful Heat Value" system to bring India closer to international practice.

## How coking coal is graded

Coking coal is graded differently, because its value is about quality for coke-making, not just heat. Indian coking coal is classified into **Steel Grades** and **Washery Grades**, based principally on **ash content** — the non-combustible mineral matter left behind. Lower ash means better, more valuable coking coal. (Raw coking coal is often **washed** to reduce its ash before use, which is why "washery grades" exist.)

## Who decides grades and prices?

For most of India's coal, grades are notified by the Coal India subsidiaries that produce it, and base prices for the regulated stream are set administratively. But a growing share of coal is sold through **e-auctions** and other market routes where the price is discovered by bidding. To make sense of all these prices together, India needed a single yardstick — and that is the National Coal Index.

## The National Coal Index (NCI)

The **National Coal Index** is a price index published by the **Ministry of Coal**. It pulls together coal prices from all sales channels — notified prices and market/e-auction prices alike — into one number that shows how coal prices are moving relative to a fixed base period (the base year is set at 100, with the index referenced to FY 2017–18).

Two features are worth understanding:

1. **It is a monthly index, not a live price.** The NCI is computed and released **monthly**. It tells you the trend in coal prices over time; it is not a ticking market quote you trade against second by second.
2. **It is composite.** The headline index is built up from sub-indices that separate **coking** and **non-coking** coal and the different mining/sales segments, so movements can be read by coal type, not just in aggregate.

The NCI has a practical purpose beyond information: it feeds into the formula for **revenue-share royalty** on coal from commercially auctioned blocks, so the index directly influences what some producers pay the government as prices move.

## Why this matters

Grades tell you *what a particular coal is worth and what it is for*; the National Coal Index tells you *which way coal prices are heading overall*. Together they are the vocabulary of the coal market — and the reason a tonne from one seam can be worth a multiple of a tonne from another.

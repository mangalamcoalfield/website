# DGMS & the rules of the mine — a regulatory overview

Every working coal mine in India operates inside a framework of law that exists for one purpose: to bring people home safely at the end of the shift. At the centre of that framework is a regulator most people outside the industry have never heard of — the **Directorate General of Mines Safety**, or **DGMS**. This article explains who they are and the main rules they enforce.

## Who DGMS is

DGMS is the national authority that administers safety in India's mines. It sits under the Ministry of Labour and Employment, and it is staffed by mining, electrical and mechanical engineers who have themselves worked in mines. DGMS does not run mines — it oversees them. Its inspectors approve equipment and certain mine plans, inspect workings, investigate accidents, grant the competency certificates that mine officials must hold, and issue the circulars and standards that interpret the law in practice.

When people in the industry say a piece of equipment is "DGMS-approved," or that a mine has "all statutory positions filled," they are referring to this body and the system it runs.

## The Mines Act, 1952 — the parent law

The foundation of mine safety law in India is the **Mines Act, 1952**. It is the parent statute: it defines what a mine is, sets duties on owners, agents and managers, regulates hours and conditions of work, requires the appointment of qualified persons, provides for inspection, and — crucially — gives the government the power to make detailed regulations under it. Almost every specific safety rule traces its authority back to a section of this Act.

## The Coal Mines Regulations, 2017 — the detailed rulebook

If the Mines Act is the constitution, the **Coal Mines Regulations, 2017** are the detailed rulebook for coal. Made under section 57 of the Mines Act, they replaced the older 1957 regulations and modernised the rules for today's mining. They run to hundreds of regulations covering, among much else:

- **Management and supervision** — who must be appointed, with what qualifications, and what each official is responsible for.
- **Ventilation** — minimum air quantities, gas and oxygen limits, and gassiness classification.
- **Support and strata control** — roof and side support, and the systematic support rules.
- **Winding, haulage and transport** — moving people and material safely.
- **Electrical and mechanical equipment** — approval, installation and testing.
- **Explosives and shotfiring**, **inundation and fire**, and the keeping of **statutory registers and records**.

Together with related rules — such as the Coal Mines (Conservation and Development) provisions, electricity rules as applied to mines, and the body of DGMS circulars — these regulations are the day-to-day law of a coal mine.

## Statutory positions: the chain of responsibility

One of the most important ideas in Indian mine law is that named, qualified individuals are personally responsible for safety. A coal mine must have a **Manager** holding the appropriate first- or second-class certificate of competency, and beneath the manager a structure of **statutory officials** — assistant managers, **overmen**, **sirdars** (mine foremen), **surveyors**, **engineers**, and, in gassier or larger mines, a **ventilation officer** and **safety officer**. Each must hold the qualification the law specifies for the role, and each carries defined statutory duties.

This is why "100% of statutory positions filled" is a meaningful claim, not a slogan. It means there is no gap in the chain of people legally accountable for the workings — every district, shift and function has a competent, certified person answerable for it.

## Registers, inspections and the paper trail

The law requires mines to keep a long list of **statutory registers and records** — of inspections, support, ventilation surveys, gas tests, equipment tests, and more. This is not bureaucracy for its own sake. A mine that records its own condition honestly and regularly is a mine that knows when something is drifting out of tolerance. DGMS inspectors examine these records, and the records are central to any accident investigation.

## How it fits together

Think of it as three layers. The **Mines Act, 1952** gives the authority and the broad duties. The **Coal Mines Regulations, 2017** (plus allied rules and DGMS circulars) give the specific, technical requirements. And **DGMS** enforces the whole structure — approving, inspecting, certifying and investigating. A compliant mine is one where all three layers are respected, every shift, by named people who are qualified to be there.

## In short

DGMS is the regulator; the Mines Act, 1952 is the parent law; the Coal Mines Regulations, 2017 are the detailed rules. Underground coal mining is permitted only on the condition that this framework is honoured in full — which is exactly why responsible operators treat statutory compliance as the baseline, not an afterthought.
